Pricing framework

Pricing core principles

North star: physical GMV and PDV revenue — keep the bill legible, list anchors honest to the market, and settlement + bundle economics visible where deals get made. One card per principle — PRICING-PRINCIPLES-AND-SCENARIOS.

1 🏪
Maximize offline channel results

Measure and monetize in-store GMV and PDV-attributed revenue — not ecommerce checkout metrics.

2 ✨
Radical simplicity for merchants

Fewer variables → clearer conversion and lighter support; the bill should read in one breath.

3 🎯
Align price communication to ICP reality

Same list can need different framing for different merchant archetypes (per PMM).

4 📊
Ground list prices in non-promotional market bands

Benchmarks must reflect sustainable table stakes — promos distort comparability.

5 ⏱️
Respect settlement horizon sensitivity

Prazo / anticipation shifts effective MDR; pricing and CRM must surface that link.

6 📈
Monitor repricing and conversion jointly

Price changes are a product surface — watch funnel and tickets by segment.

7 🔗
Bundle economics with PoS

Payments on physical GMV is the monetization engine alongside PoS SaaS — not either line in isolation.

Pricing framework · Steering discussion

Where should we put the balance?

Simplicity of the model vs economic precision — we want offline GMV, NOTR guardrails, and segment fidelity, but perfect economics on paper often implies more plans, dimensions, and exceptions. This slide frames the tradeoff, with concrete examples.

Simplicity of the model

Description

Illustrations from the working doc: CPT waiver when merchants adopt Nuvem Pago (cleaner stack, monetization shifts to spread). Invoicing inside CPT instead of a separate per-invoice line. Also: how many distinct offline / PDV commercial plans we expose — each additional plan is another story for sales, support, and the statement.

Pros

  • Faster merchant comprehension; fewer billing disputes.
  • Easier enablement — one narrative from marketing to CS.
  • Lower operational load (fewer edge cases in tooling).

Cons

  • May blur segment-level economics (who subsidizes whom).
  • Harder to tune NOTR or ARPU per micro-segment inside one simple facade.
  • Risk of leaving money on the table if simplicity caps variable upside.

Better economic output

Description

North star remains offline GMV and offline revenue; we still want NOTR guardrails, list anchors from non-promo bands, settlement/anticipation visible in quoting, and a repricing ↔ conversion loop. The tension: hitting those targets exactly on every segment often requires more granularity — more plans, riders, or exceptions — than a “one bill” ideal allows.

Pros

  • Tighter alignment to BC, acquirer costs, and segment LTV.
  • Room to reflect Upmarket vs SMB and country reality in price.
  • Clearer internal P&L ownership per lever.

Cons

  • Higher cognitive load; more “surprise” risk on the statement.
  • Support and sales friction; longer quote cycles.
  • Complexity debt — every new lever is a product surface to maintain.

Committee question: Where on this spectrum do we anchor for 2026 — maximum simplicity, maximum economic precision, or a deliberate hybrid (e.g. simple facade + controlled back-end segmentation)?
